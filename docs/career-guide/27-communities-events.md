### Pre-Event Preparation (3-4 Hours Per Event)

**Most People Waste Events by Showing Up Unprepared**

**For Conferences with Attendee Lists:**
- Download/view attendee list if available
- Filter for: Target companies, backend engineers, engineering managers, DevRel
- Identify 10-15 people you want to meet
- Prioritize: 5 "must meets", 5 "should meets", 5 "nice to meets"

**For Meetups Without Lists:**
- Check past event photos/announcements for regulars
- Look for organizers and frequent speakers
- Check LinkedIn for people who posted about the meetup

**Step 2: Personalized Outreach (1-2 hours)**

**Message Template (Send 1-2 Weeks Before Event):**
```
Subject: See you at [Event Name]?

Hi [Name],

I saw you're attending [Event Name] - I'll be there too and would love to connect!

I'm a backend engineer transitioning from pipeline operations (9 years managing SCADA systems) into Spring Boot/Java development. I've been following your work on [specific project/post/talk] and found [specific insight] really valuable.

Looking forward to meeting you there!

Best,
Leo

P.S. I'm particularly interested in [your talk topic / session you're leading].
```

**Send to:**
- 5 "must meet" people
- 2-3 speakers whose talks you plan to attend
- Event organizers (if you haven't connected yet)

### During-Event Tactics (The Art of Conference Networking)

**Tactic 1: Strategic Session Selection**

**Don't Just Attend Popular Talks**

**Criteria:**
1. **Relevant to your background:** Spring Boot, Redis, backend architecture, industrial software
2. **Presented by target company employees:** OSIsoft engineer speaking = must attend
3. **Smaller rooms = better networking:** 30-person workshop > 500-person keynote for networking
4. **Controversial/niche topics:** More memorable conversations ("I saw your talk on X...")

**Leo's Ideal Conference Day:**
- **9:00 AM:** Keynote (everyone attends - networking during breaks)
- **10:30 AM:** Small workshop on Spring Boot performance optimization (target company engineer speaking)
- **12:00 PM:** Lunch networking (sit with strangers)
- **1:30 PM:** Technical talk on Redis caching patterns
- **3:00 PM:** Networking break (focus on conversations, skip session)
- **4:00 PM:** Panel on career transitions or industrial software
- **5:30 PM:** Evening networking event/happy hour (prime time)

**Tactic 2: The "After Talk" Approach**

**Best Time to Meet Speakers:**
- **Right after their talk (2-5 minutes):** They're still at podium, you can approach
- **Breaks immediately following:** They're often in hallway near room
- **Networking events same day:** "I really enjoyed your talk earlier..."

**Approach Script:**
```
"Hey [Name], really enjoyed your talk on [topic]. The part about [specific detail] resonated with me because I dealt with similar challenges managing pipeline SCADA systems.

Quick question: [Thoughtful question that shows you understood the talk].

I'd love to stay in touch - I'm working on a Spring Boot project focused on [relevant detail]. Can I send you a LinkedIn connection?"
```

**Why This Works:**
- Shows you actually attended and paid attention
- Specific compliment (not generic "great talk!")
- Relates to your background (memorable)
- Asks specific question (demonstrates understanding)
- Clear call to action (LinkedIn connection)

**Tactic 3: The "Hallway Track" Strategy**

**Conference Secret:** The best networking happens outside the sessions.

**Where to Position Yourself:**
- **Coffee/snack stations:** People linger here, easy to strike up conversations
- **Charging stations:** Captive audience, long enough for meaningful chat
- **Sponsor booths:** Especially target company booths
- **Seating areas:** Join people who look approachable (not on phone/laptop)

**Opening Lines:**
- "What talks have you enjoyed so far?"
- "Are you speaking at this conference or just attending?"
- "How's your conference going? Any surprising talks?"
- "I'm trying to decide between [Session A] and [Session B] - have you been to either speaker before?"

**Conversation Flow:**
1. **Opening (30 seconds):** Light question about the conference
2. **Exchange backgrounds (1-2 minutes):** "What brings you here? What do you work on?"
3. **Find common ground (2-3 minutes):** Shared interests, technologies, challenges
4. **Go deeper or exchange info (2-5 minutes):** If good fit, continue conversation; if not, exchange cards politely
5. **Graceful exit:** "I need to catch the next session, but let's connect on LinkedIn!"

**Tactic 4: Meals and Evening Events (Prime Time)**

**Lunch Networking:**
- **Sit with strangers:** Don't sit with people you came with
- **Find tables with empty seats:** Join groups, don't sit alone
- **Go around the table:** Introduce yourself to everyone at table
- **Focus on 1-2 people:** Don't try to connect deeply with entire table

**Evening Events/Happy Hours:**
- **Arrive early:** Easier to join small groups than large established ones
- **Join groups of 3-4:** Easier to enter than pairs or large groups
- **Position yourself near bar/food:** Natural gathering points
- **Have an exit strategy:** "I'm going to grab another drink, but let's definitely connect!"

**Tactic 5: Target Company Booth Strategy**

**If Target Company Has Booth (OSIsoft, Uptake, Rockwell):**

**DO:**
- Visit 2-3 times during conference (not just once)
- Ask technical questions (not "are you hiring?")
- Engage with booth demos
- Talk to engineers, not just recruiters
- Get business cards from multiple people
- Mention specific products/projects you've researched

**Sample Booth Conversation:**
```
"Hi! I'm Leo. I've been following OSIsoft PI for a while - I actually used it in my previous role managing pipeline SCADA systems.

I'm curious about [specific technical question about their product].

I'm now transitioning into backend engineering (Spring Boot focus) and I'm really interested in industrial software. Would you have a few minutes to tell me about what it's like working on the engineering team at OSIsoft?"
```

**DON'T:**
- Lead with "are you hiring?"
- Take swag and leave immediately
- Just drop off resume without conversation
- Ignore the engineers and only talk to recruiters

**Follow-Up:**
- Send LinkedIn connection that evening or next day
- Reference specific conversation: "Great chatting about PI system architecture at your booth"

## Part 6: Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Mistake 1: "Networking as Extraction"

**What It Looks Like:**
- Attending events only to collect business cards
- Immediately asking for job referrals
- Not providing value before asking for help

**How to Avoid:**
- Lead with curiosity and genuine interest
- Apply 10:1 ratio (give 10x before asking)
- Build relationship first, opportunities emerge naturally

### Mistake 2: "Spray and Pray"

**What It Looks Like:**
- Attending too many events superficially
- Connecting with everyone but knowing no one
- No follow-up after events

**How to Avoid:**
- Quality over quantity: Better to attend 3 events deeply than 10 events superficially
- Pick 2-3 core communities and go deep
- Block time for follow-up (as important as attendance)

### Mistake 3: "Waiting to Be Included"

**What It Looks Like:**
- Lurking in communities without contributing
- Attending events but not starting conversations
- Waiting for organizers to notice you

**How to Avoid:**
- Take initiative: Start conversations, volunteer, create resources
- Don't need permission to add value
- Leadership is action, not title

### Mistake 4: "Networking Burnout"

**What It Looks Like:**
- Overcommitting to events and communities
- Forcing yourself to network daily when drained
- Treating networking as chore, not genuine connection

**How to Avoid:**
- Set sustainable weekly time budget (6-9 hours)
- Choose communities you genuinely enjoy
- Take breaks when needed (networking is marathon, not sprint)
- Focus on authentic connections, not transactional relationships
