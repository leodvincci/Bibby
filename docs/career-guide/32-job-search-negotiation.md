### ATS (Applicant Tracking System) Optimization

**What is ATS?**
Software that scans resumes for keywords before human sees them.

**How to optimize:**

**1. Use keywords from job description:**
If posting says "Spring Boot, PostgreSQL, Redis, Docker" â†’ your resume should include these exact terms

**2. Use standard section headings:**
- "Experience" (not "Work History")
- "Education" (not "Academic Background")
- "Skills" (not "Technical Competencies")

**3. Avoid:**
- Tables (ATS can't parse)
- Images or graphics
- Fancy fonts
- Headers/footers
- Multiple columns (use single column)

**4. Use .docx or .pdf format:**
- PDF preferred (preserves formatting)
- .docx also works well
- Avoid .pages or other formats

**5. Include exact job title:**
If applying for "Backend Engineer," use "Backend Engineer" in your summary/header (not "Software Engineer" or "Full-Stack Developer")

### Tailoring Resume for Each Application

**Don't send same resume to every company.**

**For each application:**

**1. Adjust Summary (30 seconds):**
```
Generic:
"Backend engineer with operational experience..."

For OSIsoft:
"Backend engineer with 5 years using OSIsoft PI System in production,
now building the industrial software I used to operate..."

For Datadog:
"Backend engineer with 9 years monitoring critical infrastructure,
bringing operational empathy to building observability tools..."
```

**2. Adjust Skills ordering (15 seconds):**
If job posting emphasizes Redis â†’ move Redis to top of database list

**3. Adjust project description (1 minute):**
Emphasize aspects relevant to company
- For industrial software company: Mention reliability, uptime, real-time
- For observability company: Mention monitoring, metrics, alerting

**4. Adjust experience bullets (2 minutes):**
Reorder bullets to put most relevant ones first

**Total time per tailored resume: 5 minutes**
**Worth it for Tier 1 and top Tier 2 companies**

## Part 4: The Interview Process

### Recruiter Screen (30 minutes)

**Purpose:** Verify basic qualifications, culture fit, interest level

**Questions they'll ask:**
- Tell me about your background
- Why are you interested in this role/company?
- What are you looking for in your next role?
- What's your expected salary range?
- When could you start?
- Do you have other offers/interviews?

**How to prepare:**

**1. Research company (30 min before call):**
- Recent news (funding, product launches)
- Engineering blog (recent posts)
- Glassdoor reviews

**2. Prepare your story (60 seconds):**
"I spent 9 years in pipeline operations managing SCADA systems. I built strong foundations
in systems thinking and reliability, but I wanted to transition from operating software to
building it. I completed a CS degree while working full-time, attended a bootcamp, and built
Bibby - a Spring Boot library management system. I'm now looking for a backend engineering
role where my operational background adds value. [Company] is perfect because [specific reason]."

**3. Prepare 2-3 questions:**
- "What does the team structure look like?"
- "What would my first project likely be?"
- "What's the interview process and timeline?"

**Salary question:**

**Don't give number first if possible:**
```
Recruiter: "What's your expected salary range?"

You: "I'm flexible on compensation and more focused on finding the right role and team.
What's the budgeted range for this position?"

If they push: "Based on my research for backend engineers with my background in [city],
I'm seeing ranges of $90K-$120K. Does this role fall within that range?"
```

**Research salary using:**
- Levels.fyi (most accurate for tech)
- Glassdoor (okay, often outdated)
- LinkedIn Salary Insights
- H1B database (public data for sponsored positions)

**Other offers question:**

**If you have other processes:**
"I'm in conversations with a few other companies (I don't have any offers yet), but [Company]
is definitely a top choice because [specific reason]."

**If you don't:**
"I'm early in my search and talking to a few companies. I'm being selective and focusing on
backend roles at [industrial software / observability / etc.] companies."

**Outcome:**
- Pass: Advance to technical phone screen (scheduled within 1 week)
- Fail: "We'll be in touch" (usually means rejection)

## Part 5: Offer Negotiation

### When You Get the Offer

**Recruiter will call or email:**
"We'd like to extend an offer for the Backend Engineer role. The offer includes..."

**Your response:**

**Step 1: Express enthusiasm (30 seconds)**
"That's fantastic news! I'm really excited about this opportunity and the conversations
I've had with the team."

**Step 2: Ask for details in writing (30 seconds)**
"Can you send me the full offer details in writing so I can review everything carefully?
I want to make sure I understand the full package."

**Step 3: Ask for time (30 seconds)**
"When do you need a decision by?"

**Typical deadline: 1 week** (you can often extend to 2 weeks if you have other processes)

**Step 4: DO NOT accept immediately**
"Let me review the offer details and get back to you by [date]. Thank you again!"

**Even if it's your dream job at dream salary, DO NOT accept on the spot.** Always negotiate.

### Understanding the Offer Package

**Components:**

**1. Base Salary**
- Fixed annual salary
- Paid bi-weekly or monthly
- Most important for junior/mid-level roles

**2. Equity/Stock (if applicable)**
- Startup: Stock options (ISOs or NSOs)
- Public company: RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)
- Vesting schedule (typically 4 years with 1-year cliff)
- **Value is uncertain** (especially for private companies)

**3. Bonus**
- Performance bonus (typically 10-20% of base for backend engineers)
- Sign-on bonus (one-time payment, often to offset equity you're leaving)
- **Not guaranteed** (depends on performance and company performance)

**4. Benefits**
- Health insurance (medical, dental, vision)
- 401(k) matching
- PTO (Paid Time Off) - typically 2-4 weeks
- Remote work policy
- Learning budget
- Hardware (laptop, monitor, etc.)

**Example Offer:**

```
Backend Engineer - Uptake

Base Salary: $105,000
Sign-on Bonus: $10,000
Performance Bonus: 10% target ($10,500)
Equity: 5,000 stock options, 4-year vest with 1-year cliff
Benefits: Health insurance, 15 days PTO, 401(k) 4% match, $1,500 learning budget
Start Date: May 1, 2024

Total first-year compensation: $105K + $10K sign-on + $10.5K bonus = $125,500
(excluding equity value which is uncertain)
```

### How to Negotiate

**Negotiation is EXPECTED in tech. Companies build buffer into initial offers.**

**What you can negotiate (ranked by success rate):**

**1. Base Salary (80% success rate)**
- Most common and straightforward
- Usually 5-15% increase possible

**2. Sign-on Bonus (70% success rate)**
- Easier for companies to approve (one-time cost)
- Often used to bridge gap if base is at max

**3. Equity (50% success rate)**
- More negotiable at startups than public companies
- Worth trying

**4. Start Date (90% success rate)**
- Almost always negotiable
- Gives you time to wrap up current job, relax

**5. Remote Work / Flexibility (60% success rate)**
- Increasingly negotiable post-COVID
- "Remote 2 days/week" â†’ "Remote 3 days/week"

**6. PTO (30% success rate)**
- Harder to negotiate (policy-based)
- More success at smaller companies

**What you CANNOT negotiate:**
- Benefits (health insurance, 401k match) - these are company-wide policies
- Title (usually fixed for level)

### Negotiation Script

**Step 1: Do your research (1-2 hours)**

**Use:**
- Levels.fyi - filter by company, role, location, years of experience
- Glassdoor salary reports
- Ask your network ("What's typical for backend engineer with 1 year exp at [Company]?")

**Find the range for:**
- Backend Engineer (or Software Engineer II, etc.)
- Your location (adjust for cost of living)
- Your experience level (you have 0 years software engineering, but 9 years professional experience)

**Example research:**

```
Backend Engineer in [Your City]:
- Junior (0-2 years): $85K-$105K
```

## Part 9: Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Mistake 1: Waiting Too Long to Apply

**What it looks like:**
"I'll apply when I'm 100% ready..."
Months pass, still preparing, never applying

**Why it's bad:**
- You'll never feel 100% ready
- Miss opportunities
- Lose momentum

**How to avoid:**
If you've completed Weeks 1-31 of this guide â†’ You're ready. Apply Week 32.

### Mistake 2: Spray and Pray (No Targeting)

**What it looks like:**
Apply to 100 companies with same generic resume

**Why it's bad:**
- Low response rate
- Wastes time
- Doesn't leverage your unique background

**How to avoid:**
Target 30-40 companies strategically, tailor applications to top 10-15

### Mistake 3: Not Negotiating

**What it looks like:**
Accepting first offer without discussion

**Why it's bad:**
- Leave $10K-$30K on table
- Companies expect negotiation
- Sets low baseline for future raises

**How to avoid:**
Always negotiate. Even if you love the offer, ask for 10% more.

### Mistake 4: Optimizing Only for Compensation

**What it looks like:**
Taking highest-paying offer without considering learning, team, growth

**Why it's bad:**
- First backend role is about learning, not maximizing salary
- Wrong role with $10K more isn't worth it if you're miserable or not growing

**How to avoid:**
Use evaluation framework (learning, team, impact, compensation)

### Mistake 5: Burning Bridges

**What it looks like:**
- Ghosting companies after accepting elsewhere
- Being rude to recruiters
- Bad-mouthing companies

**Why it's bad:**
- Tech industry is small
- You might want to apply there in future
- Reputation matters

**How to avoid:**
- Respond to all communications professionally
- Decline offers politely with thanks
- Keep doors open
