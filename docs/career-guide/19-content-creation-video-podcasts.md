## Introduction: The Case for Video and Audio Content

You've started writing (Section 18). Now consider video and podcasts.

**Why video matters:**

1. **Different audience:** Some developers prefer watching to reading
2. **Screen sharing:** Show your IDE, debug in real-time, walk through code
3. **Personality:** Voice, face, energy—viewers connect with you as a person
4. **Platform diversity:** YouTube has 2.5B users; not all read blogs
5. **SEO bonus:** YouTube is the #2 search engine after Google

**Why podcasts matter:**

1. **Accessibility:** People listen while commuting, exercising, cooking
2. **Long-form depth:** 30-60 minute conversations go deeper than blog posts
3. **Network building:** Guests bring their audiences to you
4. **Lower production barrier:** No video editing, just audio
5. **Growing medium:** 460M+ podcast listeners globally

**The reality check:**

Video and podcasts require more effort than writing. They have higher production barriers (equipment, editing skills, on-camera comfort). Start small, experiment, and only invest heavily if you enjoy the format.

**This week's mission:** Understand video and podcast options, choose one format to experiment with, create your first piece of video or audio content.

## Part 1: Video Content Options

You don't need to become a YouTuber. Multiple video formats exist, each with different time/skill requirements.

### Option 1: YouTube Long-Form Tutorials

**What it is:** 10-30 minute technical tutorials on YouTube

**Example topics:**
- "Building a Spring Boot REST API from Scratch"
- "Redis Caching in Spring Boot: Complete Tutorial"
- "Deploy Spring Boot to AWS: Step-by-Step Guide"

**Pros:**
- Deep SEO value (YouTube ranks in Google)
- Builds substantial authority
- Can monetize eventually (ads, sponsorships)
- Evergreen content compounds over time

**Cons:**
- High time investment (8-15 hours per video: scripting, recording, editing)
- Requires decent equipment (mic, screen recording software)
- Slow audience growth initially
- Editing learning curve

**Time commitment:** 8-15 hours per video

**Recommended frequency:** 1-2 videos per month (not weekly—too demanding)

**Best for:** Developers who enjoy teaching and have patience for slow growth

### Option 2: YouTube Shorts / TikTok / Instagram Reels

**What it is:** 30-90 second vertical videos with quick tips

**Example topics:**
- "Spring Boot tip: Use @Transactional(readOnly = true) for queries"
- "Redis caching in 60 seconds"
- "My #1 debugging trick in IntelliJ"
- "Day in the life: former Navy vet now software engineer"

**Pros:**
- Low time investment (1-2 hours per video)
- Algorithm favors short-form (potential viral reach)
- Easy to batch-record (shoot 5-10 in one session)
- No heavy editing required

**Cons:**
- Less depth (can't teach complex topics)
- Platform volatility (algorithms change)
- Harder to establish authority
- Younger audience (may not be decision-makers)

**Time commitment:** 1-2 hours per video (or 5-10 hours to batch 10 videos)

**Recommended frequency:** 2-3 per week (batch record 10-12, schedule over month)

**Best for:** Building broad audience quickly, experimenting with video before committing to long-form

### Option 3: Loom/Screencasts for Blog Posts

**What it is:** 3-8 minute screen recordings embedded in blog posts

**Example usage:**
- Blog post about Redis caching includes 5-min video showing implementation
- Article about debugging includes screencast of actual debugging session
- Post about code review includes video walking through PR

**Pros:**
- Complements written content (best of both worlds)
- Low production barrier (just record screen, minimal editing)
- Keeps readers on your blog
- Adds value without full YouTube commitment

**Cons:**
- Doesn't build separate YouTube audience
- Less discoverable than standalone videos
- Still requires decent screen recording setup

**Time commitment:** 1-2 hours per video (script, record, minimal edit, embed)

**Recommended frequency:** 1-2 per month alongside blog posts

**Best for:** Enhancing blog content without managing separate YouTube channel

### Option 4: Conference Talk Recordings

**What it is:** Record yourself giving conference-style talks, post to YouTube

**Example topics:**
- "From Pipeline Operations to Software Engineering: A Career Transition Story" (15-20 min)
- "Operational Excellence in Backend Systems: Lessons from SCADA" (20-25 min)
- "Building Real-Time Analytics with Spring Boot and Redis" (25-30 min)

**Pros:**
- Feels like practice for real conference talks (Section 21)
- Professional credibility (looks like conference content)
- Can reuse content if you give actual conference talks later
- Good for LinkedIn (professional audience)

**Cons:**
- Requires slide deck creation
- Need to be comfortable on camera
- Less casual than typical YouTube content
- Slower audience growth than tutorials

**Time commitment:** 8-12 hours per talk (slides, scripting, recording, editing)

**Recommended frequency:** 1 per quarter (4 per year)

**Best for:** Building professional credibility, practicing public speaking

### Recommendation for Leo

**Start with Option 3 (Loom/screencasts for blog posts) + experiment with Option 2 (shorts)**

**Why:**

1. **Lowest barrier:** You're already writing blog posts (Section 18). Adding 5-min screencasts is natural extension.

2. **Immediate value:** Readers of your blog get enhanced content immediately.

3. **Practice on-camera:** Shorts let you practice talking to camera without committing to 20-min tutorials.

4. **Test audience appetite:** See if your audience engages with video before going all-in on YouTube.

5. **Time-efficient:** Option 3 adds 1-2 hours to existing blog workflow. Option 2 can be batched.

**Action plan:**

- **Week 19:** Add screencast to one blog post (Article 2 from Section 18)
- **Week 20:** Create 5 short-form videos (batch record), schedule over next month
- **Week 21-24:** Continue adding screencasts to blog posts
- **Month 3-4:** Evaluate. If video resonates, invest in YouTube long-form (Option 1)

## Part 2: Podcast Content Options

Podcasts are audio-only, which lowers production barriers but requires different skills.

### Option 1: Solo Podcast

**What it is:** You talk solo for 15-30 minutes on technical topics

**Example episodes:**
- "What I learned building analytics dashboards with Spring Boot"
- "Career change lessons: 5 things I wish I knew transitioning to software"
- "How I use Anki for spaced repetition to learn algorithms"

**Pros:**
- Complete creative control
- No scheduling guests
- Can record anytime
- Low editing (mostly just trim intro/outro)

**Cons:**
- Hard to fill 20+ minutes solo (especially starting out)
- No guest audiences to tap into
- Can feel one-sided/boring
- Requires strong speaking skills

**Time commitment:** 3-5 hours per episode (prep, record, edit, publish)

**Recommended frequency:** 1 per week or biweekly

**Best for:** Confident speakers comfortable talking solo

### Option 2: Interview Podcast

**What it is:** Interview guests (developers, career changers, industry experts)

**Example episodes:**
- "Interview with OSIsoft engineer: building industrial software"
- "From military to tech: interviewing 3 Navy vets who became developers"
- "Spring Boot expert interview: advanced patterns and architecture"

**Pros:**
- Guest brings their audience
- Easier than solo (conversation flows naturally)
- Network building (build relationships with guests)
- Variety (different guests = different perspectives)

**Cons:**
- Scheduling challenges (coordinating calendars)
- Requires guest pipeline
- More editing (two audio tracks, crosstalk)
- Slow to start (need guests willing to join unknown podcast)

**Time commitment:** 5-8 hours per episode (guest outreach, prep, record, edit, publish)

**Recommended frequency:** 1 per week or biweekly

**Best for:** Networkers who enjoy conversations, have access to potential guests

### Option 3: Co-Hosted Podcast

**What it is:** You + 1-2 co-hosts discuss topics weekly

**Example format:**
- Weekly news/trends in Java/Spring Boot world
- Career advice for bootcamp grads and career changers
- Code review podcast (review open-source code together)

**Pros:**
- Built-in dynamic (easier than solo)
- Shared workload (research, editing, promotion)
- Consistency (co-host keeps you accountable)
- Conversational (listeners enjoy banter)

**Cons:**
- Need reliable co-host
- Scheduling coordination
- Shared creative control (compromise)
- Revenue split if monetizing

**Time commitment:** 4-6 hours per episode (shared across co-hosts)

**Recommended frequency:** 1 per week

**Best for:** Developers with reliable peer who's equally committed

### Option 4: Guesting on Other Podcasts

**What it is:** Don't create your own podcast—be a guest on existing ones

**Target podcasts:**
- Career change podcasts (military to tech, career pivots)
- Java/Spring Boot technical podcasts
- Industrial software/IoT podcasts
- Developer journey podcasts

**Pros:**
- Zero production work (they handle everything)
- Instant audience access (their listeners)
- Builds credibility (being selected as guest)
- Network building (relationship with host)

**Cons:**
- Need compelling story/expertise to pitch
- Hosts may say no
- Less control over final content
- Can't monetize directly

**Time commitment:** 1-2 hours per appearance (prep call + recording)

**Recommended frequency:** 1-2 per quarter (4-8 per year)

**Best for:** Developers with unique story (like Leo) who want podcast presence without creating podcast

### Recommendation for Leo

**Start with Option 4 (guesting on other podcasts)**

**Why:**

1. **Your story is compelling:** Navy → Kinder Morgan → Software is unique. Career change podcasts want this.

2. **Zero production burden:** No equipment, no editing, no publishing workflow.

3. **Instant audiences:** Podcast with 1,000 listeners gives you 1,000 potential connections.

4. **Network building:** Relationship with podcast host can lead to other opportunities.

5. **Test format:** See if you enjoy podcast format before committing to creating your own.

**Secondary option: Add audio version of blog posts**

Many blog platforms (Hashnode, Medium) support embedded audio. You can simply read your blog posts aloud, record, and embed. Gives readers option to listen instead of read.

**Time commitment:** +30 min per blog post (record yourself reading article)

## Part 3: Equipment and Setup

You don't need expensive gear to start. Focus on audio quality first.

### Minimum Viable Setup ($0-50)

**Microphone:**
- Built-in laptop mic (free, but not great)
- Wired earbuds with mic ($10-20) — significantly better than laptop mic
- Apple EarPods (if you have them) — decent quality

**Recording software:**
- **For screen recording:** OBS Studio (free, powerful)
- **For audio-only:** Audacity (free), GarageBand (Mac, free)
- **For quick screencasts:** Loom (free tier: 5-min videos), Vidyard (free)

**Editing software:**
- **Video:** DaVinci Resolve (free, professional-grade but learning curve)
- **Audio:** Audacity (free, simple)
- **Simple cuts:** iMovie (Mac), Windows Video Editor (Windows)

**Camera:**
- Built-in webcam (good enough for shorts, Zoom-quality talking head)
- Phone camera (often better than webcam, especially newer iPhones)

**Lighting:**
- Natural light from window (free, best option)
- Desk lamp pointed at wall behind monitor (bounces light onto face)

**Total investment:** $0-20 if you have laptop/phone

### Recommended Setup ($150-300)

If you commit to regular video/podcast content, invest modestly:

**Microphone:**
- **USB mic:** Blue Yeti ($100) or Audio-Technica ATR2100x-USB ($80)
- Massive audio quality improvement over built-in mics
- Plug-and-play (no audio interface needed)

**Camera (if doing on-camera video):**
- Logitech C920 webcam ($70) — industry standard for YouTube
- Or use phone with tripod mount

**Lighting:**
- Ring light ($30-50) or two desk lamps with daylight bulbs ($25)

**Boom arm for mic (optional):**
- Saves desk space, better positioning ($20-40)

**Editing software:**
- Still use free options (OBS, DaVinci Resolve, Audacity)
- Only pay for software once you're earning from content

**Total investment:** $150-250

### Recording Environment

**Background:**
- Clean, uncluttered (bookshelf, plain wall, or minimal desk setup)
- Avoid busy/distracting backgrounds
- Virtual backgrounds acceptable but less professional

**Sound:**
- Quiet room (close windows, turn off fans/AC during recording)
- Soft surfaces reduce echo (curtains, carpet, blankets)
- Closets make surprisingly good recording booths (clothes absorb sound)

**Screen recording setup:**
- Close unnecessary tabs/apps
- Zoom in on relevant code (125-150% zoom)
- Use dark theme (easier on eyes for viewers)
- Hide personal info (file paths, emails, tokens)

## Part 4: Creating Your First Video

Let's create a 5-minute screencast to accompany a blog post.

### Step 1: Choose the Topic

Pick a blog post you've already written (or are writing). Ideal topics for screencasts:

- **Code walkthroughs:** "Let me show you this Redis caching implementation"
- **Debugging sessions:** "Here's how I debug N+1 queries"
- **Tool demonstrations:** "How I use IntelliJ shortcuts for refactoring"
- **Project tours:** "Quick tour of the Bibby codebase"

**For Leo's Week 19:**

Blog post: "Refactoring a Messy Spring Boot Controller: Before and After"
Screencast: 5-minute walkthrough showing the actual refactoring in IntelliJ

### Step 2: Script the Outline

Don't write word-for-word script (you'll sound robotic). Write bullet-point outline:

**Example outline for controller refactoring screencast:**

```
0:00-0:30 — Intro
- "Hey, I'm Leo. This is a quick walkthrough of refactoring a messy Spring Boot controller"
- "Starting with 200 lines, multiple responsibilities. Ending with clean 50-line controller"

0:30-1:30 — Show the problem
- Open BookController.java (before refactoring)
- Scroll through, point out issues:
  - Business logic in controller (lines 45-78)
  - No validation (line 23)
  - Poor error handling (line 89)

1:30-3:30 — Show the refactoring
- Open refactored controller side-by-side with original
- Walk through changes:
  - Moved business logic to BookService
  - Added @Valid for validation
  - Created GlobalExceptionHandler
- Show how each change improves code

3:30-4:30 — Show the result
- Run application, test endpoint with Postman
- Show validation working (send invalid request)
- Show error handling (trigger 404)

4:30-5:00 — Wrap up
- "Controller went from 200 lines to 50, single responsibility"
- "Full code on GitHub [link in description]"
- "Blog post has more details [link]"
```

**Goal:** 5 minutes covers one topic with enough depth to be useful, not so much it's overwhelming.

### Step 3: Set Up Recording

**OBS Studio setup (screen + webcam):**

1. Download and install OBS Studio (free)
2. Create new scene
3. Add sources:
   - Display Capture (your screen) or Window Capture (just IDE)
   - Video Capture Device (webcam, optional—can do screen-only)
4. Position webcam in corner (if using)
5. Test audio levels (speak normally, aim for -12 to -6 dB)

**Alternative: Loom setup (easier):**

1. Install Loom browser extension or desktop app
2. Click record
3. Choose: screen only, screen + camera, or camera only
4. Click start recording
5. Done—no manual setup needed

**Recommendation:** Start with Loom (easier). Graduate to OBS if you commit to regular video.

### Step 4: Record

**Before you hit record:**

- [ ] Close distracting tabs/apps
- [ ] Clear notifications (Do Not Disturb mode)
- [ ] Zoom IDE to 125-150% (readable on small screens)
- [ ] Open all files you'll reference
- [ ] Test audio (record 10 seconds, play back)

**Recording tips:**

1. **Do a dry run:** Walk through outline once without recording
2. **Speak slightly slower than normal:** Clarity > speed
3. **Pause between sections:** Easier to edit out pauses than add them
4. **Don't aim for perfection:** Small mistakes are fine, edit them out or leave them (humanizing)
5. **If you mess up badly:** Pause 2 seconds, restart that section. Don't stop entire recording.

**Energy level:**

You need more energy on camera than in person. What feels like "too much" energy usually plays as "normal" on video. Smile, use inflection, sound interested in your own content.

### Step 5: Edit

**Minimal editing (30-60 min):**

1. **Trim intro/outro:** Cut silence at beginning/end
2. **Remove long pauses:** Cut dead air (but leave natural pauses for pacing)
3. **Cut major mistakes:** If you said something wrong and corrected it, cut the mistake
4. **Add simple title card:** 3-5 seconds at start with your name and topic
5. **Add end screen:** 3-5 seconds with "Thanks for watching" + links

**Tools:**

- **Loom:** Auto-trims silence, no manual editing needed (easiest)
- **DaVinci Resolve:** Professional but learning curve
- **iMovie/Windows Video Editor:** Simple, good for basic cuts

**Don't over-edit:**

You're not making a Hollywood film. Viewers tolerate imperfection. Done > perfect.

### Step 6: Publish and Embed

**If using Loom:**
1. Click "Share"
2. Copy embed code
3. Paste into blog post (Hashnode/dev.to support embeds)

**If exporting to file:**
1. Export as MP4 (1080p, H.264 codec)
2. Upload to YouTube (can be unlisted if you just want embed link)
3. Embed YouTube link in blog post

**Blog post integration:**

Place video after introduction, before main content:

```markdown
# Refactoring a Messy Spring Boot Controller

When I first built Bibby's BookController, it was 200 lines of mixed
responsibilities. Here's how I refactored it to clean architecture.

**Watch the 5-minute walkthrough:**

[embedded video]

Now let's dive into the details...
```

Gives readers option to watch or read (or both).

## Part 5: Creating Your First Short-Form Videos

Short-form video (YouTube Shorts, TikTok, Reels) is higher-risk, higher-reward.

### Content Ideas for Shorts

**Quick tips:**
- "Spring Boot tip: Always set readOnly=true on query transactions"
- "My favorite IntelliJ shortcut: Cmd+Shift+A for action search"
- "Redis caching explained in 60 seconds"

**Behind the scenes:**
- "Day in my life as a career-change software engineer"
- "Debugging a production issue at 11 PM"
- "My dev setup tour"

**Career content:**
- "3 lessons from transitioning Navy → software engineering"
- "Why I built Bibby as my portfolio project"
- "How I study algorithms: Anki spaced repetition"

**Code reviews:**
- "Reviewing my own code from 6 months ago (yikes)"
- "Before and after: refactoring messy code"

### Recording Short-Form Video

**Vertical video (9:16 aspect ratio):**
- Use phone camera (easier than webcam)
- Hold phone vertically
- Or record horizontal and crop in editing

**Two formats:**

**Format 1: Talking head**
- You on camera, talking directly to viewer
- Can show screen recordings as B-roll
- More personal, builds connection

**Format 2: Screen recording with voiceover**
- Just screen capture, no face
- Easier if camera-shy
- Works for quick code tips

### Batch Recording Process

Don't record one video at a time. Batch record 10-12 videos in one session.

**Batch recording day (4-6 hours):**

1. **Prep (1 hour):**
   - Write outlines for 10-12 videos
   - Set up recording space (lighting, tripod, background)
   - Gather props if needed (laptop, code examples)

2. **Record (2-3 hours):**
   - Record all 10-12 videos back-to-back
   - Don't edit—just record raw footage
   - 2-3 takes per video maximum

3. **Edit (1-2 hours):**
   - Trim to 30-90 seconds
   - Add captions (critical for shorts—80% watch without sound)
   - Add simple text overlays for emphasis
   - Export all

4. **Schedule (30 min):**
   - Upload to YouTube Shorts, TikTok, Instagram Reels
   - Schedule: 2-3 per week over next month
   - Write captions, add hashtags

**Result:** 4-6 hours of work produces 1 month of content (3 videos/week × 4 weeks = 12 videos)

### Editing Short-Form Video

**Mobile editing (easiest):**
- **CapCut:** Free, easy, auto-captions, trending templates
- **InShot:** Simple editing, good for trimming and text overlays

**Desktop editing (more control):**
- DaVinci Resolve (free)
- Adobe Premiere Pro (paid, $21/month)

**Key edits:**
1. **Trim to time:** 30-90 seconds maximum
2. **Add captions:** Auto-generate (CapCut, YouTube Studio), then correct errors
3. **Hook in first 3 seconds:** Start with interesting statement or visual
4. **Text overlays:** Emphasize key points
5. **Music (optional):** Use copyright-free music from YouTube Audio Library

### Promotion Strategy

**Cross-post everywhere:**
- YouTube Shorts
- TikTok
- Instagram Reels
- LinkedIn (if professional content)
- Twitter/X (can embed video)

**Hashtags matter for shorts:**
- #SpringBoot #Java #Backend #Coding #SoftwareEngineer
- #CareerChange #MilitaryToTech (if applicable)
- #LearnToCode #100DaysOfCode

**Engage with comments immediately:**
- Algorithm rewards fast engagement
- Reply to all comments in first hour

**Experiment with timing:**
- Post 7-9 AM or 5-7 PM (when developers scroll before/after work)
- Test different days

Leo
[LinkedIn profile]

**Why this works:**

1. **Personalization:** You listen to the podcast (specific episode reference)
2. **Unique angle:** Navy → pipelines → software (not generic "career change")
3. **Value proposition:** Clear bullet points on what you'd discuss
4. **Social proof:** Blog/content showing you can articulate ideas
5. **Easy yes:** Flexible on timing, not demanding

### Preparing for Podcast Interview

**Pre-interview prep (2-3 hours):**

1. **Listen to 3-5 recent episodes:**
   - Understand format, tone, typical questions
   - Note host's interview style

2. **Prepare your stories:**
   - 3-5 key anecdotes you can tell in 2-3 minutes
   - Career transition moments
   - Technical challenges from Bibby
   - Lessons from Navy/Kinder Morgan

3. **Outline talking points:**
   - Not a script, just bullet points
   - Key messages you want to convey
   - Stats, numbers, metrics (make stories concrete)

4. **Test equipment:**
   - Mic check, quiet room
   - Headphones (prevent echo)
   - Stable internet if remote

5. **Send host any materials they need:**
   - Bio (50-100 words)
   - Headshot (if video podcast)
   - Links (blog, GitHub, LinkedIn)

**During interview:**

- **Energy up:** Same as video—more energy than normal conversation
- **Tell stories, not facts:** "When I was managing pipeline SCADA at Kinder Morgan..." beats "I worked at Kinder Morgan"
- **Be concise:** 2-3 minute answers, not 10-minute monologues
- **Ask host questions too:** Make it conversational
- **Mention your work naturally:** "...which is why I built Bibby to demonstrate these patterns"

**After interview:**

- Send thank-you email to host
- Share episode on your LinkedIn, Twitter when it publishes
- Engage with comments on podcast platforms
- Tag the host when sharing (brings their audience to you)

## Part 7: Measuring Success (Video/Podcast)

Video and podcast metrics differ from blog metrics.

### Video Metrics (YouTube, Shorts)

**Views:**
- How many people watched?
- Short-form: 1,000-10,000 views possible if algorithm favors you
- Long-form: 100-500 views realistic for first few videos

**Watch time / Average view duration:**
- More important than views
- If people watch 80%+ of your video, algorithm promotes it
- If people drop off at 10%, algorithm buries it

**Click-through rate (CTR):**
- % of people who see thumbnail and click
- 4-6% is good for new channels
- Indicates compelling thumbnail/title

**Engagement:**
- Likes, comments, shares
- Comments especially valuable (signals quality to algorithm)

**Subscribers:**
- Slow growth at first (10-50 subscribers in first month is normal)
- Compounds over time

**What good looks like:**

- **First video:** 50-200 views, 2-5 comments, 40-60% watch time
- **After 5 videos:** 500-1,000 total views, 20-50 subscribers
- **After 3 months (12-15 videos):** 2,000-5,000 total views, 100-200 subscribers, some videos getting recommended

### Podcast Metrics (if hosting)

**Downloads per episode:**
- 50-100 downloads first episode is normal
- Grows slowly (10-20% month-over-month)

**Listener retention:**
- What % listen to completion?
- 50%+ is good (many people start podcasts, don't finish)

**Subscriber growth:**
- Podcast apps track subscriptions
- Slow growth (10-30 subscribers/month early on)

**What good looks like:**

- **First episode:** 50-150 downloads in first month
- **After 10 episodes:** 200-400 downloads per episode
- **After 6 months (20-25 episodes):** 500-1,000 downloads per episode

### The Long Game

Video and podcast growth is slower than blog growth initially, but can compound faster later.

**Timeline:**

- **Months 1-3:** Slow growth, learning production, finding voice
- **Months 4-6:** Some videos/episodes start performing, small audience building
- **Months 7-12:** Compounding kicks in, algorithm favors consistent creators
- **Year 2+:** Established audience, opportunities emerge (speaking, sponsorships, job offers)

**Don't quit too early.** Most creators quit after 5-10 videos/episodes because growth is slow. Those who persist to 50-100 pieces of content see results.

## Part 8: Time Management and Sustainability

Video and podcast take more time than writing. Be realistic.

### Weekly Time Budget

If you're working full-time + MBA + building Bibby, time is limited.

**Realistic allocation (Week 19 onward):**

- **Writing (Section 18):** 8-10 hours/week (1 article)
- **Video/Podcast:** 3-5 hours/week (1 screencast OR batch shorts OR guest appearance)
- **Total content creation:** 12-15 hours/week

**That's sustainable.**

**What's NOT sustainable:**

- Writing 2 articles/week + 2 YouTube videos + podcast = 25-30 hours (burnout)

### Choosing Your Focus

You can't do everything. Pick 2 primary channels:

**Option A: Writing-focused**
- Primary: 1 blog post/week
- Secondary: 1 screencast/month embedded in blog
- Result: Strong blog presence, occasional video enhancement

**Option B: Writing + Short-form video**
- Primary: 1 blog post/week
- Secondary: 3 shorts/week (batched monthly)
- Result: Blog for depth, shorts for reach

**Option C: Writing + Podcast guesting**
- Primary: 1 blog post/week
- Secondary: 1 podcast appearance/month (guest, not host)
- Result: Blog for SEO, podcast for network building

**Recommendation for Leo: Option A or B**

Writing is your foundation (Section 18). Add video only if it doesn't compromise writing consistency.

**If you enjoy video:** Option B (shorts are low-effort, high-reach)
**If you don't enjoy video:** Stick with writing, add occasional screencast

**Avoid:** Starting your own podcast (too time-intensive right now). Guest appearances are fine (low time commitment).

## Part 9: Common Video/Podcast Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Expensive Equipment Before Validation

**Wrong:** Buy $500 mic, $300 camera, $200 lights before creating anything

**Right:** Start with phone/laptop mic, create 5-10 videos, upgrade if you're sticking with it

**Why:** You need to validate you enjoy the medium first. Many people buy gear, create 2 videos, quit.

### Mistake 2: Over-Editing

**Wrong:** Spend 10 hours editing 15-minute video to perfection

**Right:** Spend 1-2 hours on basic edits (trim, title card, end screen)

**Why:** Diminishing returns. Jump from 80% to 95% quality takes 5x the time. Not worth it early on.

### Mistake 3: No Script/Outline

**Wrong:** Hit record and ramble for 30 minutes, hope it's coherent

**Right:** Spend 20 minutes outlining bullet points, then record focused 10-15 minute video

**Why:** Viewers' time is valuable. Tight, focused content beats meandering rambles.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Audio Quality

**Wrong:** Great video, terrible audio (laptop mic in echoey room)

**Right:** Decent video, great audio (USB mic, quiet room)

**Why:** Viewers forgive mediocre video but not bad audio. Audio quality matters more than visual quality.

### Mistake 5: Inconsistent Publishing

**Wrong:** Publish 5 videos in week 1, then nothing for 2 months

**Right:** Publish 1 video/week for 10 weeks

**Why:** Algorithms reward consistency. Audiences expect consistency. Batch recording helps maintain schedule.

### Mistake 6: Copying Others' Style

**Wrong:** Try to replicate Fireship's fast-paced humor or TechLead's sarcasm

**Right:** Be yourself—former Navy/pipeline operator bringing operational perspective to code

**Why:** Your differentiation is your background. Lean into it, don't hide it trying to sound like typical YouTubers.

### Mistake 7: No Call to Action

**Wrong:** Video ends abruptly, no links, no next steps

**Right:** End every video with "Full code on GitHub [link], follow me on LinkedIn [link], see you next week"

**Why:** Viewers need clear next step. Don't assume they'll find you—tell them how.
